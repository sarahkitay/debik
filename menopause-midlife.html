Menopause & Midlife

Midlife and menopause can be a time of meaningful transition.

For many women, this phase brings physical changes alongside deep questions about identity, worth, desire, and direction. Therapy can offer a place to make sense of what's unfolding without reducing it to symptoms or asking you to push through.

Beliefs That Often Accompany This Phase

Many women arrive believing that their femininity is behind them, that they are less desirable, less capable, or somehow not enough. Others assume they should stay silent, manage it alone, or push through, as if needing support means they are failing.

This is not how I understand menopause or midlife.

These stories are deeply reinforced by culture and by years of responsibility carried without pause. They deserve examination.

What is surfacing now is often meaningful.

What This Phase Often Brings to the Surface

Midlife is frequently a time of cumulative stress and re-evaluation. Many women are simultaneously facing changing bodies, caregiving roles, work demands, and shifting relationships, often with fewer internal and external resources than before.

As familiar roles loosen, questions about identity, agency, and meaning tend to emerge more insistently. This can feel disorienting, unsettling, or strangely clarifying all at once.

Perimenopause, menopause, and postmenopause can intensify this process, not because something is wrong, but because the body and nervous system are less able to buffer what has long been managed.

Common Experiences I See in This Work

Women often describe:

A sense of losing control or no longer recognizing themselves
Emotional reactivity tied to sleep disruption, stress load, or physical changes
Shifts in desire, body image, or self-trust
Resurfacing grief or unresolved experiences
Dissatisfaction with work, relationships, or long-held expectations

These experiences are not signs of failure. They are often signals that something important is asking to be reconsidered.

How Therapy Can Help

In therapy, we work to understand these changes within a broader psychological and relational context. This may include making sense of identity shifts, addressing grief or resentment that has gone unspoken, and exploring how you want to relate differently to yourself and others moving forward.

What This Work Isn't

This work is not medical treatment, hormone management, or coaching toward a "reinvented" version of yourself. It is also not limited to trauma or crisis, although past experiences may become more visible.

Who This Is For

This work may be a good fit if you identify as a woman and are navigating perimenopause, menopause, or postmenopause and find yourself questioning roles, relationships, or identity in ways that feel unfinished or unresolved.

Many people I work with are caregivers, professionals, or long-time capable people who are realizing that earlier ways of coping no longer fit.
